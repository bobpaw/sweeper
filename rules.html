Minesweeper, as she as played

Here is an imaginary game to help teach you:

  • Starting out, you decide to reveal the center cell.
  • In the above 3x3 grid, the number 3 represents the number of mines around it.
  • Exactly 3 of the other cells must have mines. However, you don't necessarily know which cells contain mines.
  • You decide to guess and click the top left cell.
  • Because it has no surrounding mines, it automatically reveals the surrounding cells.
  • Looking at the one, you can't immediately tell which adjacent cell has a mine. But wait! The 2 only has 2 adjacent cells! You flag both.
  • Now you may feel really stuck. However, because the 1 has two adjacent cells, you can think slightly quantum. (Though really it's more statistics.) Look at it as though the 1 is in both. This is really thinking about both possiblities as equally true.
  • In both scenarios, the 3 is now fulfilled. This leaves the bottom right cell, which must not be a mine!
  • The 1 is already fulfilled by the flag above it, so the last mine must be in the bottom left.
  • Now, you have two choices. The game is ended by clicking all non-mine cells. Flagging is for your own benefit. You may either click every non-mine cell, or flag everything and then do that to win.

3BV is used to calculate objective board score or the minimum number of clicks needed to win.
